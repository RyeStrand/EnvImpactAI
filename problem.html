THE PROBLEM

The environmental toll of AI, particularly large language models (LLM) like ChatGPT, is becoming increasingly difficult to ignore. Training GPT-3, for instance, required 1,287 megawatt hours of electricity—enough to power roughly 120 U.S. homes for an entire year—and resulted in the emission of around 552 tons of carbon dioxide (Zewe 2025). These energy demands aren’t just limited to the training phase; even day-to-day usage comes with a heavy footprint. A single ChatGPT query uses about five times more electricity than a basic web search, indicating the computational intensity behind generating responses (Zewe 2025). Supporting this level of energy consumption requires robust cooling systems, and on average, two liters of water are needed to cool the equipment for every kilowatt hour used (Zewe 2025).
This demand for water has already had an impact on local communities. In West Des Moines, Iowa, where a major data center cluster supports OpenAI’s most advanced model, GPT-4, residents discovered through a lawsuit that the facility used about 6% of the district’s total water supply in just one month, right before GPT-4’s training was completed. Similar patterns have emerged elsewhere, with Google and Microsoft reporting sharp spikes in water usage—20% and 34% increases, respectively—as they ramped up AI development. As usage of these systems expands globally, the environmental footprint grows with it. One analysis estimates that ChatGPT’s ongoing operations already consume as much electricity as 33,000 homes (Crawford 2024).
Looking ahead, global AI-related water withdrawals are projected to reach between 4.2 and 6.6 billion cubic meters by 2027—exceeding the annual water usage of countries like Denmark or equaling half of the United Kingdom’s. These figures emphasize the need to weigh AI’s benefits against its demands (Li 2023).
